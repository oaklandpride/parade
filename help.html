Parade Contingent Volunteers

For the efficient and safe execution of the Parade, each contingent is required to appoint one contingent monitor for every 25 people in the contingent, and two wheel monitors for every basic vehicle in the contingent. For large trucks, floats, buses, and other vehicles over 10 feet, increase the number of wheel monitors to four (4).

This means that a contingent of 100 people and one vehicle would need four contingent monitors and two wheel monitors. A contingent of 50 people and two vehicles would need two contingent monitors and four wheel monitors.

  1. Contingent monitors are responsible for ensuring that the contingent stays in formation and follows the Parade route. They also help to keep the contingent safe by monitoring the crowd and making sure that everyone is staying a safe distance from the vehicles.
  2. Wheel monitors are responsible for ensuring that marchers do not become caught in the wheels of a vehicle. They stand along either side of a vehicle, and must be in place at all times a vehicle is in motion. They should stand an arm's-length away on each side of a basic vehicle, or an arm's length away parallel to the axles. Drivers may not move their vehicles unless wheel monitors are in place.

The contingent monitors help to keep the Parade moving smoothly, and the wheel monitors help to prevent accidents. This ensures that everyone involved in the Parade can enjoy it safely and without incident.

Here are some additional things to keep in mind when appointing contingent monitors and wheel monitors:

  • Contingent monitors should be people who are familiar with the Parade route and the location of all vehicles.
  • Wheel monitors should be people who are familiar with the operation of vehicles and capable of keeping people away from the wheels.
  • Both contingent monitors and wheel monitors should be able to communicate effectively with the drivers of vehicles and the participants in the Parade.
  • Both contingent monitors and wheel monitors should be able to remain calm and focused under pressure.

By following these guidelines, you can help to ensure that the contingents in your Parade are well-organized and safe.

Non-Contingent Volunteers

Parade volunteers can be identified by their yellow vests.

Volunteer Shifts

Name Shift Time Role Description
Assembly 8:45am to 11:30am greet contingents, assist with check-in, hand out order placards
Route 10:45am to 1pm meter gaps between contingents with hand signals, escalate issues over radio
Dispersal 10:30am to 1pm direct the flow of vehicle traffic off site, and marchers into the celebration
ADA Parade Viewing 10am to 1pm direct users to available seats, ensure seats are made available for accessibility needs

Radio Etiquette

If you have been equipped with a two-way radio, use these guidelines for effective communication.

Speak clearly and slowly when using your radio.

Be aware of the noise level around you. If there is a lot of noise, you may need to speak closer to the microphone or turn away from the sound.
